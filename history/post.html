YearOccurence
450 BC Plato reported a dialogue in which Socrates asks Euthyphro for an algorithm to distinguish pieth from non-peity
1847 George Boole introduced his formal language for making logical inference, Boole's approach was incomplete, but good enough that others filled in the gaps
1879 Gottlob Frege produced a logic that, except for some notational changes, forms the first-order logic that is used today as the most basic knowledge representation system
1900 David Hilbert presented a list of 23 problems that he correctly predicted would occupy mathematicians for the bulk of century. The final problem asks if there is an algorithm for deciding the truth of any logical preposition involving the natural numbers - the famous Entscheidungs problem, or decision problem. Essentially, Hilbert was asking if there were fundamental limits to the power of effective proof procedures
1930 Kurt Godel showed that there exist an effective procedure to prove any true statement in the first-order logic of Frege-Russell; but first order logic could not capture the principle of mathematical induction needed to characterize the natural numbers
1931 Kurt Godel showed that real limits do exist. His incompleteness theorem showed that in any language expressive to describe the properties of the natural numbers, there are true statements that are undecidable: their truth cannot be established by any algorithm
1940 The first operational modern computer was the Heath Robinson, built by Alan Turing's team for the single purpose of deciphering German messages
1941 The first operational programmable computer was the Z-3, the invention of Konard Zuse in Germany. Zuse invented floating point no for Z-3
1942 In the United States, the first electronic computer, the ABC, was assembled by John Atanasoff and his graduate student Clifford Berry between 1940 and 1942 at Iowa State University
1943 The first work that is now generally recognized as AI was McCullouch and Pitts' 1943 formal design for Turing-complete "artificial neurons"
1945 Zuse developed Plankalkul, the first high level programming language
1952 Nathaniel Rochester and his group build IBM 701, this was the first computer to yield income for its manufacturer
